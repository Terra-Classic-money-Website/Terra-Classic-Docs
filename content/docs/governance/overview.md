Governance is the decision layer of Terra Classic.

It is how staked LUNC is used to approve, reject, fund, signal, or execute changes that affect the chain. Governance can influence protocol parameters, software upgrades, tax and fee rules, community pool spending, product directions, and public coordination.

Governance is not a company board, a customer support desk, or a guarantee that every passed idea will be delivered well. It is a token-weighted control process. That makes participation powerful, but it also makes voter quality, validator accountability, and proposal discipline extremely important.

## Who this section is for

Use this section if you are:

- a LUNC holder trying to understand what governance can change
- a delegator who wants to know how validator votes affect your stake
- a community member deciding how to vote on proposals
- an investor evaluating whether Terra Classic governance is serious
- a user trying to avoid unsafe, vague, or overpromised proposals

Developers should also read the [governance module specification](/develop/module-specifications/spec-governance/) when they need protocol-level details.

## What governance can affect

Governance can affect several parts of Terra Classic:

| Area | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Protocol upgrades | Software upgrade proposals, module changes, chain halt coordination. |
| Parameters | Deposit, voting, tax, fee, staking, slashing, and module settings where governance-controlled. |
| Community funding | Community pool spend proposals and public-good funding. |
| Product direction | Signaling proposals for L1 products such as Swap Protocol or Forex Protocol. |
| Public coordination | Roadmaps, policies, working groups, and other off-chain commitments. |

Not every proposal executes automatically. Some proposals change chain state directly. Others are text or signaling proposals that require humans, teams, or validators to follow through after the vote.

## The core idea

Terra Classic governance has three connected layers:

1. Staked LUNC creates voting power.
2. Validators vote by default with delegated voting power.
3. Delegators can override their validator's vote by voting directly before the deadline.

This means staking and governance are connected, but they are not the same thing. Staking explains how voting power is created. Governance explains how that power is used.

## Why governance quality matters

Weak governance can damage a chain even when the chain keeps producing blocks.

Poor governance can lead to:

- low-turnout decisions
- validator non-participation
- vague funding requests
- untested protocol changes
- rushed software upgrades
- repeated proposal churn
- weak accountability after a proposal passes

Strong governance does the opposite. It makes decisions easier to inspect, improves user trust, gives serious builders clearer requirements, and makes it harder for weak proposals to pass on hype alone.
