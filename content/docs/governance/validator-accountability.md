Validators are not only block-signing operators.

They are also default governance representatives for delegators who do not vote directly. That makes validator accountability a governance issue, not only a staking issue.

## Minimum governance expectations

A responsible validator should:

- vote on governance proposals consistently
- explain important votes
- prepare for software upgrades
- communicate incidents
- disclose material conflicts
- keep commission behavior transparent
- maintain useful public contact information
- avoid misleading delegators
- support chain safety over short-term attention

This is the baseline. It is not an exceptional contribution.

## Non-voting is still a signal

If a validator repeatedly does not vote, that is not neutral.

It means the validator is carrying delegated voting power without using it. This weakens representation and pushes decisions toward a smaller active set.

Delegators should treat repeated non-voting as a serious accountability problem.

## Vote explanations

Validators should explain important votes, especially when proposals affect:

- L1 upgrades
- tax and fee parameters
- community pool spending
- validator economics
- oracle behavior
- market or swap modules
- stablecoin-related proposals
- security-critical changes
- ecosystem funding

A short explanation is often enough. The point is to make the decision inspectable.

## Conflicts of interest

Conflicts are not automatically disqualifying. Hidden conflicts are the problem.

A validator should disclose material conflicts where relevant, such as:

- operating services that benefit from a proposal
- running applications affected by a proposal
- receiving funding from a project involved in a vote
- maintaining infrastructure that governance may fund
- promoting another chain or product that competes with Terra Classic priorities

Delegators do not need perfect purity. They need clarity.

## Communication standards

A validator should make it easy to find:

- official website or profile
- contact channel
- governance vote rationale
- incident updates
- upgrade readiness notices
- commission information
- public contributions

Silence is costly. Delegators cannot evaluate risk if validators do not communicate.

## Accountability tools for delegators

Delegators can respond to weak validator behavior by:

- voting directly
- asking validators to explain votes
- tracking voting records
- redelegating away from passive validators
- splitting stake across multiple credible validators
- avoiding validators that increase concentration risk
- supporting validators that publish useful public information

Redelegation is not drama. It is how delegated proof-of-stake creates accountability.

## Validator accountability scorecard

| Criterion | Strong signal | Weak signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Voting | Regular participation. | Frequent non-voting. |
| Rationale | Explains major votes. | Silent on important proposals. |
| Conflicts | Discloses relevant conflicts. | Hidden or ignored conflicts. |
| Communication | Clear public channels. | No useful contact or updates. |
| Delivery | Shows concrete contribution. | Slogans without proof. |
| Safety | Supports review and evidence. | Pushes rushed high-risk changes. |
| Delegator respect | Makes risk understandable. | Treats delegation as passive income. |
